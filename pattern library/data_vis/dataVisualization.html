Data Visualization

Palette

Data is the key factor to making informed decisions. Therefore, it is important for us to interpret and present data correctly and clearly. Below are a few standards that we have set with these principles in mind.

Charts

Stylistically, our charts follow a clean, new, and fresh design. All chart data should be in the 'Georgia' (serif) typeface, and all colours should be complementary.

Line Graphs

Utilizes a series of connected points linked by lines to illustrate variations in data values.

The horizontal axis represents a continuous progression, usually referring to the passage of time.

The vertical axis displays data values related to a specific metric of interest, relating to the progression on the horizontal axis.

Use solid lines to display actual data trends, and dotted for showing the average or the projection.

Lines should be 2pts in weight.

Bar Graphs

Utilizes either horizontal or vertical bars to present data related to a certain category.

One axis of the chart represents the categories under comparison, while the other axis represents a defined scale of values.

Use straight corners, and ensure equal spacing between bars.

Single-Value Charts

Utilizes specifically designed cards and circular progress bars, paired with dynamic movement signals to effectively capture the viewer's attention and guide their focus toward key performance indicators.

Cards should be used to display important static numbers, and ongoing trends.

Use 🡅 to represent positive trends and 🡇 to represent negative trends.

Circular progress bars should be used to display percentages related to progress.

Area Charts

An extension of a line graph, illustrating the variation in numeric values for one or more groups as they relate to a secondary variable.

Depicts changes in volume or quantity over a defined time frame, offering a sense of accumulation within quantitative data.

Particularly valuable for conveying the overarching trend or pattern within a dataset.

Well-suited for visualizing multiple data series that involve part-to-whole relationships or cumulative series of values.

Scatter Plot

Visualizes the relationship between two variables.

Used for showcasing data distribution and clustering tendencies.

Points should be 5px by 5px.

When using multiple categories, be sure to use different shapes in order to differentiate them. General order is: circle, diamond, triangle, square, upside-down triangle.

The chosen data should be able to be highlighted and brushed on the scatter plot.

Pie Chart

Presents the connections between a variable's components and its overall structure.

The sum of the values along the circumference must always total 100%.

Discouraged, as they can make it difficult for users to interpret the data clearly.

Donut Chart

Much like a pie chart but includes a hole in the center, which is used for data or labels.

Can be a more visually pleasing alternative to pie charts.
